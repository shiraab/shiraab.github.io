Shira is a writer, translator, and full-stack software engineer currently working at Studio. She also writes and translates with Limited Connection Collective. In recent years, she has been an editor, a Civic Digital Fellow, a barista, an intern, and a responsible computing teaching assistant. She recently graduated from Brown University with degrees in Translation and Computer Science.

CS111 is Brown CS's newest introductory computer science course, which blends computing foundations and data science. I was in charge of creating a coherent curriculum that would teach all students, regardless of political leanings or past experience with data ethics, to think about the ways in which data and software affect the world, and the power that they, as data scientists, would soon have in their grasp. In addition to curriculum development and evaluation, I also led grading – which was no small task in a class of 300 students. We now teach students about data privacy, harmful assumptions in data encoding, stakeholders and decision-making, and a little bit about policy. This project was part of Brown CS's Socially Responsible Computing initiative.

The following semester, I co-led the responsible computing integration for CS15, Brown CS's oldest running introductory computer science course, taught by Andy van Dam. In a team of two, I was in charge of adding a segment on responsible computing in the news to each lecture – which included everything from the January 6th hearings with Meta to ongoing issues of algorithmic bias to Dr. Timnit Gebru's firing from Google.

Working at the intersection of web development, design, software engineering, and translation, I helped revamp Formally's website.

After years of collaborating on experimental poetry projects, Rachel Landau and I (aka The Legs of the Corduroy Pants) founded and created Poems from a University Quarantine as a place to stay connected and creating during the long months away from school. As site manager, I managed its accessibility and organization. We ran a two-part workshop series over Zoom with the Brown Arts Initiative called POETRY IS FOR KITCHENS. Our summer 2020 programming, POETRY IS FOR SUMMERS, included a summer workshop for high school and college students and a reading series which featured poets such as Cassidy McFadzean, Domenica Martinello and Mónica de la Torre. This project was supported by a grant from the Brown Arts Initiative .

Web accessibility is notoriously rare, and many web developers say that this is because they were never taught how to implement it. As TAs for our department's only Web Development course, I was hired to create an all-new accessibility and ethics curriculum. The course schedule is extremely tight, since students learn everything from HTML basics to frameworks like Vue and React to database management to mobile development. The solution? Instead of creating new assignments, I took existing assignments and asked what component of accessible web development could be taught through it, starting with simple principles like alt text and WAI-ARIA landmarks and going up to dynamic text resizing and skip links. This project was part of Brown CS's Socially Responsible Computing initiative. As part of my work as a Brown CS Health and Wellness Advocate, I worked with courses and with the department as a whole to ensure that their respective sites meet web accessibility standards.

We gathered, analyzed, and visualized data based on a central question: did the scandals surrounding the 2016 election and big tech companies change tech PACs' donation patterns?

We worked with The Policy Lab to create a tool for Wagtail CMS-based sites to automatically submit published pages for translation through a third-party API.

Place-memory can be loosely described as the set of historical, cultural, and social ideas relating to a particular place. I planned, led, and conducted a semester-long foray into place-memory in literature and history in Paris, culminating in a French-language website.

We worked with an on-campus newspaper to build a map that allows site editors to add articles to a map, connecting writing to the place where it is based.

In late 2018, I conducted a research project about Nazi looted art at the Museum of Fine Arts Boston. I corresponded with Dr. Victoria Reed, Provenance Curator at the MFA, whose research helped me identify two paintings in the collection with particularly interesting backstories. After spending several days at the museum writing, looking, and taking film photographs, I produced a long-form lyric essay, which I printed and hand-bound.
